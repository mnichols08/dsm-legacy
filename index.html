THE PARTNERSHIP

How two automotive giants joined forces to create a legend

A Groundbreaking Alliance

Diamond Star Motors (DSM) represented a pioneering cross-continental partnership between America's Chrysler Corporation and Japan's Mitsubishi Motors. The name ingeniously combined elements from both companies' logos: Mitsubishi's three diamonds and Chrysler's pentastar.

Formed in 1985, this joint venture established its manufacturing home in Normal, Illinois, with a state-of-the-art facility spanning nearly 2 million square feet. The plant incorporated cutting-edge automation with approximately 1,000 robots working alongside its human workforce.

What made DSM remarkable was its blend of Japanese engineering precision with American automotive design sensibilities. This cultural fusion resulted in vehicles that captured the imagination of enthusiasts and everyday drivers alike, creating a legacy that continues to influence automotive culture today.

The Chrysler-Mitsubishi Partnership Before DSM

Before the Diamond Star Motors joint venture officially formed in 1985, Chrysler and Mitsubishi had already established a strong working relationship that produced several notable vehicles. These early collaborations laid the groundwork for what would eventually become one of the most significant Japanese-American automotive partnerships.

1982-1989

These vehicles featured cutting-edge technology for their time and earned a reputation in motorsports.

The Technology Transfer

The partnership between Chrysler and Mitsubishi allowed for significant technology sharing that benefited both companies. Mitsubishi's advanced engineering expertise in areas like turbocharging, all-wheel drive systems, and performance tuning helped Chrysler compete in segments where they previously lacked competitive products.

Captive Imports Era

Before Diamond Star Motors, Chrysler imported Mitsubishi vehicles and rebadged them for the American market. These "captive imports" helped Chrysler fill gaps in their lineup, particularly in the compact and subcompact segments, while giving Mitsubishi access to the American market through Chrysler's established dealer network.

Performance Heritage

Vehicles like the Starion/Conquest demonstrated Mitsubishi's commitment to performance engineering. Their turbocharged engines, advanced suspensions, and motorsport success created a performance legacy that directly influenced the development of DSM's iconic Eclipse, Talon, and Laser models.

Beyond DSM

Even after Chrysler sold its stake in Diamond Star Motors, the partnership continued through vehicles like the Dodge Stealth (rebadged Mitsubishi 3000GT) and later collaborations on platforms that underpinned vehicles like the Dodge Stratus coupe and Chrysler Sebring coupe, which were based on Mitsubishi designs.

The origins of Diamond Star Motors began when Chrysler acquired a 15% stake in Mitsubishi Motors. This initial partnership allowed Chrysler to distribute Mitsubishis as captive imports under Chrysler, Dodge, and Plymouth brands.

Chrysler was now importing 110,000 Mitsubishis annually. The compact cars were helping Chrysler meet consumer demand for smaller, more fuel-efficient vehicles, filling a critical gap in their product lineup.

Chrysler Corporation and Mitsubishi Motors Corporation officially established Diamond Star Motors as a 50-50 joint venture. The partnership was designed to circumvent import quotas and combine resources for mutual benefit.

Construction began on a $650 million manufacturing facility in Normal, Illinois. The 1.9 million square foot plant was designed with an annual production capacity of 240,000 vehicles, showcasing both companies' commitment to the venture.

The Diamond Star Motors manufacturing facility was completed in Normal, Illinois. The highly automated plant featured approximately 1,000 robots working alongside its human workforce.

The first "DSM triplets" rolled off the assembly line: the Mitsubishi Eclipse, Plymouth Laser, and Eagle Talon. These sporty 2+2 coupes shared the same platform with different styling cues and would soon develop a cult following.

Facing financial difficulties, Chrysler sold its 50% stake in the joint venture to Mitsubishi. However, the production of Chrysler vehicles continued on a contractual basis, maintaining the partnership in practice.

Diamond Star Motors was officially renamed Mitsubishi Motors Manufacturing America (MMMA), reflecting Mitsubishi's full ownership of the facility. Despite the name change, many enthusiasts continued to use the DSM designation.

Production of the Eagle Talon ended as Chrysler discontinued the Eagle brand entirely. The DSM trilogy was reduced to a duo with only the Mitsubishi Eclipse continuing production for future generations.

Mitsubishi announced the closure of the plant in Normal, Illinois. Despite efforts to find a buyer to preserve jobs, the facility that had been the birthplace of the DSM legends would close its doors after nearly three decades of operation.

THE VEHICLES

The iconic models that defined the Diamond Star Motors legacy

The DSM Triplets

The three vehicles produced at the Diamond Star Motors plant in Normal, Illinois shared the same platform, drivetrain options, and basic engineering, but each carried unique styling and brand identities. Known collectively as "the DSM triplets" among enthusiasts, these sports coupes offered impressive performance at an accessible price point.

Technical Specifications

Powertrain

The top-tier models featured the legendary 4G63 turbocharged 2.0L inline-four engine, producing 195 horsepower and 203 lb-ft of torque. This robust engine became famous for its tuning potential, with enthusiasts routinely extracting 300+ horsepower with basic modifications.

All-Wheel Drive

The AWD system on GSX, TSi AWD, and RS Turbo AWD models featured a viscous coupling center differential that sent 50% of power to the rear wheels in normal conditions, with the ability to transfer more power to either axle as needed for optimal traction in various conditions.

Performance

The turbocharged AWD variants could accelerate from 0-60 mph in approximately 6.4 seconds - impressive performance for early 1990s sports cars in their price range. Their combination of speed, handling, and all-weather capability made them formidable competitors against more expensive sports cars.

THE FACTORY

The heart of Diamond Star Motors production

Advanced Facility

The Normal, Illinois plant spanned an impressive 1.9 million square feet and represented a $650 million investment. This state-of-the-art facility was designed to produce up to 240,000 vehicles annually, showcasing the commitment both Chrysler and Mitsubishi had to the joint venture.

Automation Pioneer

The factory featured cutting-edge automation with approximately 1,000 robots working alongside human workers. This blend of high technology and skilled labor created a manufacturing powerhouse that set new standards for efficiency and quality.

Workforce Excellence

At its peak, the plant employed nearly 3,000 workers who formed the United Auto Workers Local 2488 in 1989. The combination of a skilled workforce and innovative manufacturing processes contributed to the quality and performance that made DSM vehicles so beloved.

Production Versatility

The factory demonstrated remarkable flexibility, producing multiple models on the same production line. Beyond the initial DSM triplets, the facility would go on to manufacture other vehicles including the Mitsubishi Galant and later the Mitsubishi Outlander Sport.

THE LEGACY

The enduring impact of Diamond Star Motors

An Enthusiast Phenomenon

Though the Diamond Star Motors partnership was relatively short-lived in automotive terms, its impact on car culture has been profound and enduring. The term "DSM" evolved beyond the company name to become shorthand among automotive enthusiasts for the Eclipse, Talon, and Laser models.

These vehicles developed a devoted following for their tunability and performance potential. The 4G63 turbocharged engine found in top-trim models proved capable of handling significantly more power than stock, making DSM vehicles favorites in the aftermarket tuning community.

Racing success further cemented the DSM legacy, with modified Eclipses, Talons, and Lasers competing successfully in various motorsport disciplines from drag racing to hill climbs. In 2004 and 2005, Greg Collier won the NASA Super Unlimited class national title in a Plymouth Laser RS Turbo, beating purpose-built Ferrari and Porsche race cars.

Today, well-preserved examples of first-generation DSM vehicles are increasingly sought after by collectors, recognizing their significance in automotive history as pioneers of accessible performance and emblems of Japanese-American automotive collaboration.
